Project: GapMinder¶

Investigating 4 key indices across all the countries of the world¶

1. Introduction¶

GapMinder is a dataset compiled by the GapMinder foundation that tracks key indices (like population, GDP etc.) across different countries of the world. In this dataset I am investigating 4 key indices- life expectancy, GDP per capita, child mortality and fertility across 193 different countries of the world. The data is compiled from 1800-2018.

Index explaination
1) Life expectancy is the average life in years of all the people in that country. An epidemic or a war can cause more deaths and reduce this statistic.
2) GDP per capita is the gross domestic product / population of the nation in US dollars.
3) Child mortality is number of deaths / 1000 births in a country.
4) Fertility rate is number of births per woman in a country.

In this project I will be analyzing the following-
1) World wide trend for all 4 key indices since 1800 to 2018.
2) Exploring any unsual trends in the 4 indices over time.
3) Exploring if there is any correlation between the 4 indices.
4) Finding which countries performed best in each index and comparing them with major world economies- USA and China.
5) Visualizing the GDP of major economies for comparison.

For this project I have used Numpy, Pandas, Matplotlib and Seaborn libraries.

2.3 Transforming dataframes¶

Since each dataset has about 220 columns (one for each year), I will be transforming the data into Year, Country, Index format. This type of dataframe would be much easier to use for comparisons later on.

The below function transforms each dataframe passed to it into format: Year,Country, Index (3 columns).

In [533]:
# Making a function for transforming the Dataframes into format: Year,Country, Index (3 columns).

def df_transform(df,index_num):
    
    #Finding the size of the DataFrame
    dim0 = np.size(df,axis=0)
    
    # my_indices[index_num] is the third column name
    my_indices= ['child_mortality','fertility', 'gdp', 'life_exp']
    print(my_indices[index_num])
    df2= pd.DataFrame()
    
    # Loop transposes all rows in the DataFrame into columns and concatenates them over each other
    
    for i in range(dim0):
        df1= df.iloc[i,1:].reset_index() # Transposing the row to column format making index (year) as seperate column
        
        #Making a DataFrame with country name as a new column by using dictionary and changing data type to int and float
        temp= pd.DataFrame({'year': df1['index'].astype(int),'country':df.iloc[i,0],\
                            my_indices[index_num]:df1.iloc[:,-1].astype(float) })
        
        # Concating the temporary 
        df2 = pd.concat([df2,temp], axis=0)
        
    return df2

3.2 Finding Correlations between Indices¶

Seaborn library was used to find coorelation between the 4 indices. Pearson's coefficient is also calculated to quantify the correlation.

Observations
1) It can be seen that there is a strong negative correlation between child mortality and life expectancy.( Pearson's coefficient: -0.99). The underlying cause might be better healthcare facilities in those countries which lead to both higher life expectancy and lower child mortality.
2) There is a strong postive correlation between GDP and life expectancy (coefficient: 0.96) which shows that richer countries enjoy longer life.
3) Child mortality and fertility have postive correlation of 0.9. This could possibly be that parents have less kids if the child has higher chance of survival to adulthood. However, there might be other factors in play here.
4) Higher GDP nations have less fertility rates (coefficient: -0.96).

From the time plots it is clear that child mortality rate is falling with time with sharp fall from 1900s onwards. Additionally, fertility rate also saw a sharp fall from 1950s while GDP per capita and life expectancy saw a rise since that period.

Moreover, there is a strong negative correlation between child mortality and life expectancy and between GDP per capita and fertility rates. Additionally, there is a strong positive correlation between GDP and life expectancy and between fertility and child mortality.
